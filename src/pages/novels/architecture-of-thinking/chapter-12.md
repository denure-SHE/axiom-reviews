## Unwinding in the SHE Network


Neha’s meeting had run longer than expected.

By the time she walked into the café near Ananya’s office, her shoulders carried the weight of a conversation that had needed too much care — too many sentences measured, too many pauses watched.

“Tough one?” Ananya asked, turning her phone face down on the table.

Neha exhaled, the sound somewhere between a laugh and a sigh. “Very. Everything felt like negotiation. Every sentence.”


Ananya smiled — not in surprise, but in recognition.


“Work. Home. Everywhere,” she said. “It’s all negotiation.”

That made Neha laugh too — relief more than humor.


They ordered without looking at the menu. The café was quiet in the way weekday evenings are quiet — laptops closed, bags still open, people not quite done with the day.

“For a moment,” Neha said, stirring her coffee, “I wondered if I was pushing too hard. Or not enough. It’s exhausting, deciding where to hold and where to let go.”


Ananya nodded. “You don’t stop,” she said. “You just get better at choosing what’s worth it.”


Before Neha could respond, Ananya’s phone vibrated. She glanced at it and smiled faintly.

“Maid,” she said. “Asking what to cook.”


She answered without hesitation. “I’ll be home in an hour. Dal is fine. Keep it simple.”

No checklist. No follow-up.

She ended the call and slipped back into the conversation.


Neha had been watching. “You don’t even think about it, do you?” she said.

Ananya smiled. “Oh yes ... If the basics are working, you don’t need to keep watching. You just adjust when needed.”


Neha had been watching.

“You don’t even think about it, do you?” she said.

Ananya smiled.
“If the basics are steady, I don’t keep checking. I step in when something changes.”

“You didn’t ask what’s done.”

“There’s no point redoing what already works,” Ananya said.

She paused.

“If the rhythm holds, you let it run.”

* * *

Neha tilted her head.

“And you actually do that?” she asked, half teasing. “At work?”

Ananya raised an eyebrow. “I try.”


Neha smiled, a little knowingly.

“I think you let go,” she said gently, “only after you’re sure nothing will fall.”


The sentence landed. Because it was true.

Ananya didn’t deflect. “I’m working on that,” she said. “Knowing it is easy. Doing it is not.”


Neha nodded. “When roles are clear, conflict drops,” she said. “People don’t fight clarity.”

Ananya smiled faintly. “It’s the same at home,” she said. “Mira doesn’t argue much about structure. It’s predictable.”

She paused, then added, “The rest… I usually absorb.”

Neha looked at her carefully. “And that holds?” she asked.

Ananya didn’t answer immediately.

“We love our children so much that we want to carry everything for them,” Neha continued gently. “Sometimes it’s healthy to delegate too. Or just… let go of some invisible ownership.” 


Ananya stared at the condensation on her glass. “My mental calendar is wired to hold everything,” she said slowly. “If I let go, I worry something small will slip — and then I’ll have to pick up the pieces. That's again a headache , I like less of that ! ”


Neha smiled. “Sometimes nothing slips,” she said. “You just stop holding it.”

That stayed.


“You looked tired last weekend,” Neha added quietly.


Ananya nodded. “I was. Not physically. Just… holding too many things at once.”

She thought of the museum. Of stepping back. “Letting go feels like loss at first,” she said.


Neha nodded. “And then you realize ..Voila ! nothing breaks.”


They sat in silence for a moment. Not heavy. Just understood.

* * *

When they stood to leave, Neha said, “People have been talking about your work.”

Ananya glanced at her.

“It’s good work,” Neha added. “It’s going to get louder before it settles.”


Ananya understood. “I know.”


Neha studied her for a second.

“You don’t need things to be easy,” she said. “Just clear.”

They parted without ceremony.

* * *


As Ananya walked toward her car, her phone vibrated again.

She didn’t check it immediately.


For a moment, she let the day stay where it was.


Nothing was collapsing.

Not at work , Not at home.

Things weren’t perfect. But they were holding.

She started the car. Tomorrow would be louder.

But tonight, there was quiet.

And that was enough.
